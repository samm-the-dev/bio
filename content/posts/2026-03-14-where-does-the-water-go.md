A [recent post on this site](/blog/does-ai-really-use-that-much-energy) compared the energy footprint of AI-assisted development to PC gaming. The energy picture, while worth understanding, is relatively abstract -- kilowatt-hours distributed across a global grid. Water tells a different story. Water stress is hyper-local, and the communities affected are specific, identifiable, and often too small to effectively push back. Data centers have been straining local water supplies for over a decade -- long before AI entered the picture. AI is accelerating that pattern, and doing so fast enough that communities are struggling to keep up.

## The hidden multiplier

When data center companies report water usage, they typically report WUE (Water Use Effectiveness) -- direct on-site cooling water. What generally goes unreported is the water consumed by power plants generating their electricity.

According to [LBNL](https://eta-publications.lbl.gov/sites/default/files/2024-12/lbnl-2024-united-states-data-center-energy-usage-report_1.pdf), U.S. data centers consumed roughly 17 billion gallons of water directly for cooling in 2023. The indirect water consumed by power generation was an estimated **211 billion gallons**. The total: ~228 billion gallons, or about 0.19% of U.S. water withdrawals. [Siddik et al. (2021)](https://iopscience.iop.org/article/10.1088/1748-9326/abfba1), a Virginia Tech/LBNL study, validated this ratio independently and found that one-fifth of U.S. data centers' direct water footprint comes from moderately to highly stressed watersheds -- and nearly half of servers are powered by plants in water-stressed regions.

Nationally, that 0.19% is small. U.S. agriculture withdraws roughly [118 billion gallons per day](https://pubs.usgs.gov/publication/cir1441) -- over 1,300 times more than AI data centers consume in a year. But water stress isn't national, and the growth trajectory is steep: according to LBNL, AI GPU-accelerated server electricity [grew from under 2 TWh in 2017 to over 40 TWh in 2023](https://eta-publications.lbl.gov/sites/default/files/2024-12/lbnl-2024-united-states-data-center-energy-usage-report_1.pdf), while overall data center electricity grew at 7-18% annually over the same period. The [DOE projects](https://www.energy.gov/articles/doe-releases-new-report-evaluating-increase-electricity-demand-data-centers) U.S. data center electricity could reach 325-580 TWh by 2028. If those projections hold, the water commitments being locked in today will shape these communities for decades.

According to available sustainability reports, that 12x multiplier between direct and indirect water is almost entirely absent from corporate disclosures. Only [Meta](https://sustainability.atmeta.com/asset/2025-environmental-data-index/) appears to report it: over 55,000 megalitres of embedded water in purchased electricity in 2024 -- roughly 14x their direct withdrawal. [Google](https://sustainability.google/reports/google-2025-environmental-report/), which publishes [facility-level water data](https://sustainability.google/reports/google-2025-environmental-report/#section-water) for each of its data centers, still omits indirect water entirely.

The indirect footprint also suggests that grid decarbonization is grid _de-watering_. Solar and wind use effectively zero water per MWh, while coal with cooling towers consumes 400-700 gallons and nuclear 600-800 per [NREL estimates](https://docs.nrel.gov/docs/fy11osti/50900.pdf). The difference matters for siting: according to [EPA eGRID data](https://www.epa.gov/egrid), Google's Council Bluffs, Iowa facility sits on one of the dirtiest grids in the country at 1,480 lb CO2/MWh -- and is also its most water-intensive facility at 1.3 billion gallons per year. A [Cornell study](https://news.cornell.edu/stories/2025/11/roadmap-shows-environmental-impact-ai-data-center-boom) found that smart siting combined with faster grid decarbonization could cut data center water impacts by 86%.

## Three towns, three stories

### The Dalles, Oregon

The Dalles is a city of about 15,000 on the Columbia River. Google's data center there consumed [434 million gallons in 2024](https://www.opb.org/article/2026/01/15/as-googles-water-demands-grow-the-dalles-aims-to-pull-more-from-mount-hood-forest/) -- roughly **a third of the city's total water supply**. That's up 316% from 104 million gallons in 2012.

The city cannot draw from the Columbia River due to environmental law. It depends on aquifers capped at 10 million gallons per day, and resident well water levels are declining year over year.

When the city tried to understand how much water Google was using, [Google and the city sued to keep the data secret](https://fortune.com/longform/google-data-center-the-dalles-oregon-water-dispute/). They lost in 2022 after a district attorney ruling. In Council Bluffs, Iowa, Google [attempted to annex county land](https://insideclimatenews.org/news/04032026/google-attempts-to-annex-iowa-county-land-for-data-center/) to avoid new water requirements for another data center expansion.

The city is now pursuing an expanded reservoir from the Hood River basin on Mount Hood -- but that basin is drought-stressed and provides habitat for ESA-listed coho salmon, Chinook salmon, and steelhead.

At the basin level, The Dalles registers as LOW water stress on the [WRI Aqueduct Water Risk Atlas](https://www.wri.org/aqueduct). But the city can't access the basin's primary resource (the Columbia), so the local reality is HIGH stress. This gap between basin-level metrics and local experience is a recurring pattern in data center siting.

### Memphis, Tennessee

Memphis is the largest U.S. city that relies exclusively on groundwater. The Memphis Sand Aquifer supplies the city's drinking water. [USGS has documented](https://www.usgs.gov/publications/hydrology-aquifer-systems-memphis-area-tennessee) water-level declines of 0.1 to 1.3 feet per year since 1928, with regional declines of up to 70 feet. Contamination pathways from urban development are documented, and arsenic threatens drinking water near the xAI facility.

xAI's Colossus data center -- [the largest known single-site GPU cluster](https://www.theverge.com/2024/9/3/24234737/xai-colossus-cluster-100000-nvidia-gpus) with over 200,000 Nvidia GPUs -- currently draws 1.3 million gallons per day from the aquifer, with plans to scale to over 5 million gallons per day. The company broke ground on an $80 million water recycling plant in October 2025, but [it will only reduce aquifer strain by 9%](https://www.fox13memphis.com/news/xai-breaks-ground-on-80m-xai-water-recycling-plant/article_29064c97-abda-4c85-b51b-4562361cfb3a.html).

[Community groups describe South Memphis](https://www.technostatecraft.com/p/how-south-memphis-became-a-sacrifice), where the facility is located, as a "sacrifice zone." The NAACP is lead plaintiff in legal challenges in both Tennessee and Mississippi. This is the same neighborhood that bears the brunt of xAI's [unpermitted air pollution](https://www.selc.org/news/xai-built-an-illegal-power-plant-to-power-its-data-center/). At xAI's second facility in nearby Southaven, Mississippi, the [NAACP, SELC, and Earthjustice](https://earthjustice.org/press/2026/naacp-threatens-lawsuit-over-xais-unpermitted-gas-turbines-in-mississippi) filed a 60-day notice to sue under the Clean Air Act in February 2026 over 27 unpermitted gas turbines. Air quality and water impacts compound in the same community: Memphis consistently ranks among the top three [Asthma Capitals](https://aafa.org/asthma-allergy-research/our-research/asthma-capitals/) in the U.S. according to the Asthma and Allergy Foundation of America.

### Dona Ana County, New Mexico

OpenAI's Project Jupiter, a [$500 billion partnership](https://openai.com/index/announcing-the-stargate-project/) with Oracle and SoftBank, is planned for Dona Ana County. [USGS documents](https://pubs.usgs.gov/sir/2019/5145/) a groundwater deficit averaging 1,480 acre-feet per year from 1953 to 2014. New Mexico's overall groundwater supply is [projected to shrink 25-30% by 2050](https://www.ose.nm.gov/WPB/50YrPlan/).

The Mesilla Basin aquifer serves Las Cruces, El Paso, and Ciudad Juarez across two countries. The Rio Grande Compact already creates interstate disputes between New Mexico, Texas, and Mexico over water allocation. The county is already in [EPA nonattainment](https://www.epa.gov/green-book) for both PM10 (since 1991) and ozone (since 2018). Project Jupiter's estimated 14+ million tons of GHG per year would be layered on top of existing environmental burdens. The [New Mexico Environmental Law Center](https://nmelc.org/) has filed two lawsuits, and a [$165 billion bond issuance](https://sourcenm.com/2026/02/28/critics-of-proposed-stargate-data-center-project-in-dona-ana-county-have-filed-a-lawsuit/) is being challenged for violating the Open Meetings Act.

## "Water positive" and its discontents

Several companies have pledged to become "water positive" by 2030 -- replenishing more water than they consume. But water isn't fungible the way carbon is sometimes treated.

Google's Mayes County, Oklahoma facility withdraws [1.1 billion gallons per year](https://www.readfrontier.org/stories/as-data-centers-boom-in-oklahoma-so-does-water-demand/) and discharges 253 million gallons of wastewater into the Neosho River. Google has pledged to [replenish 120% of freshwater by 2030](https://sustainability.google/reports/google-2025-environmental-report/) and reached 64% in 2024, up from 18% in 2023 -- but its replenishment projects are in _different watersheds_ from where the facility draws. [Privette et al. (2026)](https://agupubs.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1029/2025AV002140?af=R) argue that current water disclosure is insufficient to evaluate whether replenishment claims are meaningful, and that these pledges are "beginning to face the same skepticism that carbon offsets now encounter."

## What's actually promising

**Zero-water cooling** is real and deploying. [Microsoft's next-generation AI data centers](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/microsoft-cloud/blog/2024/12/09/sustainable-by-design-next-generation-datacenters-consume-zero-water-for-cooling/) use direct-to-chip liquid cooling that consumes zero municipal water, saving an estimated 125,000 cubic meters per facility per year. [Oracle](https://www.oracle.com/news/announcement/blog/closed-loop-cooling-in-oracle-ai-data-centers-2026-02-09/) is deploying similar closed-loop systems. This matters because it means water consumption can be fully **decoupled** from energy growth -- even if [efficiency gains get absorbed by expanded usage](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.16548) on the energy side, water doesn't have to follow.

**Grid decarbonization reduces water use simultaneously.** Since solar and wind consume effectively zero water per MWh, shifting data centers from coal/gas grids to renewable grids eliminates most of the indirect water footprint -- the 12x multiplier that almost no company reports.

**State regulation is emerging.** Minnesota [became the first state](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/laws/2025/1/12/) to enact data center water regulation in June 2025, requiring separate water appropriation permits and $2-5 million annual fees for facilities using over 100 million gallons per year. Florida's [SB 484](https://floridaphoenix.com/2026/03/13/florida-senate-half-heartedly-agrees-to-final-ok-for-weakened-data-center-bill/) passed both chambers on March 13. Over [300 data center bills](https://www.multistate.us/insider/2026/3/13/local-data-center-regulations-gain-ground-as-state-bills-falter) have been filed across 30+ states in 2026, and [19+ Michigan municipalities](https://planetdetroit.org/2026/03/lawmakers-propose-michigan-data-center-moratorium/) have enacted local moratoriums. Southern Nevada [banned evaporative cooling](https://www.reviewjournal.com/local/henderson/henderson-council-votes-to-ban-evaporative-cooling-in-future-projects-2725890/) for new businesses outright.

**Efficiency is improving.** [Meta's](https://sustainability.atmeta.com/asset/2025-environmental-data-index/) WUE reached 0.18 L/kWh in 2024, with a fleet-wide PUE of 1.08 (industry average is 1.56 per Uptime Institute). [Amazon's](https://sustainability.aboutamazon.com/) WUE reached 0.15 L/kWh in 2024, down 40% since 2021. Air-cooled facilities (Google in Dublin, Montreal, Storey County NV) use near-zero water. The technology to build water-efficient data centers exists -- the question is whether it gets deployed fast enough and in the right places.

## An aside: how we got here

AI didn't create the data center water problem. A decade of cloud computing, streaming, social media, and enterprise IT built the infrastructure -- and the water dependencies -- that exist today. [Goldman Sachs Research estimates](https://www.publicpower.org/periodical/article/ai-drive-165-increase-data-center-power-demand-2030-goldman-sachs) current global data center power at about 55 GW, with AI accounting for just 14% of workloads. The other 86% is the cloud and enterprise computing that was already there. The [IEA's 2025 report](https://www.iea.org/reports/energy-and-ai/energy-demand-from-ai) independently puts AI at about 15% of total data center energy.

But AI is changing the trajectory. Goldman Sachs projects total data center demand will reach 84 GW by 2027, with AI nearly doubling its share to 27%. [McKinsey estimates](https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/technology-media-and-telecommunications/our-insights/ai-power-expanding-data-center-capacity-to-meet-growing-demand) ~70% of new data center capacity by 2030 will serve AI workloads. The water stress documented in the communities above wasn't caused by AI -- but AI is the force multiplier accelerating new construction into those same watersheds, at a pace that outstrips the regulatory and infrastructure capacity of small cities to respond.

## The pattern

The pattern that emerges from these cases is that water impacts are local, concentrated, and tend to fall on communities with limited political leverage -- small cities in Oregon, South Memphis, border communities in New Mexico. In Northern Virginia, the largest U.S. data center cluster, [Loudoun County water use reportedly rose 250% in four years](https://www.bayjournal.com/news/growth-development/in-a-data-center-hub-even-the-smallest-leaks-can-t-be-spared/article_79cbebcc-b5d8-11ef-9e3c-5324c89e5e4b.html), drawing from a Potomac River basin that supplies drinking water for 6 million people.

To be clear about what the data does and doesn't show: the [2026 U.S. drought](https://droughtmonitor.unl.edu/) -- over 42% of the country as of March -- is driven by [La Nina](https://www.drought.gov/news/el-nino-southern-oscillation-and-drought-outlook-united-states-2025-09-08), the [second-warmest U.S. winter on record](https://yaleclimateconnections.org/2026/03/we-just-had-the-second-warmest-winter-in-u-s-history-despite-icy-blasts/), and [snowpack collapse](https://www.drought.gov/drought-status-updates/snow-drought-current-conditions-and-impacts-west-2026-02-05) across the West. No water authority has cited data center consumption as a factor in any current restriction. Data centers are not why anyone is rationing showers.

But that may not be the most useful question. The more interesting one is whether a fast-growing industry should be locking in decades of water commitments in already-stressed watersheds without adequate transparency. According to available reporting, no company breaks out AI-specific water metrics separately from general data center operations. Of the seven major AI companies, three -- Anthropic, OpenAI, and xAI -- [publish no environmental data at all](https://www.eenews.net/articles/states-push-to-end-secrecy-over-data-center-water-use/). The data that exists is fragmented across sustainability reports, [USGS studies](https://www.usgs.gov/mission-areas/water-resources/science/total-water-use), local news, and legal filings. Even the companies that do report face questions about methodology: Microsoft [switched its water consumption estimation approach](https://datacenters.microsoft.com/sustainability/efficiency/) between FY2023 and FY2024, the same period it reported a consumption decline, and an [MDPI analysis](https://www.mdpi.com/2071-1050/16/23/10197) found that selective accounting methodology can make Big Tech environmental footprints appear far smaller than location-based measurements suggest.

The question isn't whether AI data centers caused the drought. They didn't. Data centers have been drawing from these watersheds for years, and AI currently accounts for a fraction of that load. But AI is what's driving the next wave of construction -- and doing so faster than the communities hosting these facilities can evaluate the long-term cost. The question is whether those communities and aquifers can sustain the demand being placed on them, and whether the industry growing fastest into them is being transparent enough about its impact.
